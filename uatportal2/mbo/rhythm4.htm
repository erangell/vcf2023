RHYTHM

Meter

In rhythm, not all notes are created equal, even ones with the same duration. In order to create a rhythmic "feel" or pulse in the music, some notes get more intensity, called accent, than others. There are even gradations within the accented and nonaccented categories. The grouping of accented and unaccented notes is called meter. In most music of the western world, meter is divided into two categories: simple and compound.

Simple Meter

To establish a rhythm, one note value has to be designated as the beat (sometimes called the unit or beat unit). Everything else in the rhythmic grouping is a division or multiple of that beat. In simple meter, the beat is divided into two equal parts (or the equivalent) where the accent is on the first of the two parts. In compound meter, the beat is divided into three equal parts (or the equivalent) where the accent is on the first of the three parts.

The most common duration used to represent the beat in simple meter is the quarter note. The most common duration used to represent the beat in compound meter is the dotted quarter note.

Regarding simple meter, as you can see in the example below, the quarter note is the beat (unit). When the quarter note is divided into two equal parts, each part is equal to an eighth-note division of the beat. You’ll recall that eighth notes have a single flag extending from the stem of the note, a sixteenth note two flags and so on.
