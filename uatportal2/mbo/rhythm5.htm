RHYTHM

We typically show all the durations that equal one beat through beamed groups of successively smaller durations, or subdivisions, of the beat. Eighth notes are represented by one beam. When the quarter note is divided into four equal parts, each part is equal to a sixteenth-note subdivision of the beat represented by four double-beamed notes. When the quarter note is split into eight equal parts, each part is equal to a thirty-second-note represented by eight triply-beamed notes. When the quarter note is divided into sixteen equal parts, each part is equal to a sixty-fourth note represented by quadruply-beamed notes. Since the sixty-fourth note is the smallest practical duration used in music, we really can’t show a subdivision greater than sixteen equal parts if the quarter note is the beat. If the half note were the beat, the lowest possible level would equal a subdivision into 32 sixty-fourth notes. In other words, the number of possible subdivisions is determined by the length of the beat.

Music is usually organized to make it easier to determine the beat unit by grouping subdivisions into a single beat. For example, the following illustrates a combination of an eighth note and two sixteenth notes which, when we add them all up, equal one quarter-note beat.
