MAJOR SCALES

Scale degrees

As you have learned, a basic scale beginning on C is major. Look carefully at the sequence of whole and half steps in the major scale: 1 - 1 - ½ - 1 - 1 - 1 - ½ .

Each note of a major scale, or any seven-note diatonic scale for that matter, is called a degree, and each degree has a name that relates to its function within the scale. The function names are important because they reveal how the notes of a diatonic scale are related to each other. If you continue beyond this course, you will find that chords share the same function names as the scale degrees upon which they are built. The example below shows the C major scale with the function names associated with the scale degrees.

The most important note of a diatonic scale is the first degree or TONIC note (abbreviation = T). The tonic note defines the tonality of a piece of music.

The second most important note of a diatonic scale is the DOMINANT or fifth degree of the scale (abbreviation = D). The term comes from Gregorian chant of antiquity where the fifth degree often was the most commonly used, or dominant, note of the chant melody even though the tonic was often the first and last note.

The second degree or SUPERTONIC (abbreviation = S) is the note directly above (the prefix “super” means above) the tonic.

The third degree is called the MEDIANT (abbreviation = M) because it is midway (“median” means midway point) between the tonic and dominant notes.

The fourth degree of the scale is called the SUBDOMINANT (abbreviation = SD) because it has the same relationship below the tonic as the dominant has above the tonic. The dominant is five lines and spaces above the tonic. The subdominant is five lines and spaces below ("sub" means below) the tonic.

The sixth degree of the scale is called the SUBMEDIANT (abbreviation = SM) because it is midway between the tonic and subdominant.

The seventh degree of the scale is called the LEADING-TONE (abbreviation = LT) because in a major scale, it usually moves (LEADS) up to the tonic note when used in a piece of music.

With one exception, the function names described above are used for the scale degrees of all seven-note diatonic scales. That exception is the leading-tone. You will notice that in the major scale, there is a half-step between the seventh degree and the tonic. In any mode where the seventh degree is a half-step from the tonic, the function name is called a leading-tone. However, in scales where there is a whole step between the seventh degree and the tonic (e.g., Dorian), the function name of the seventh degree is called the SUBTONIC (the same relationship below the tonic as the supertonic is above the tonic (abbreviation = ST).
