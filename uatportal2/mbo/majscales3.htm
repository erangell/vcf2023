MAJOR SCALES

Constructing major scales

We can construct major scales on any of the 12 notes in the octave simply by using the same interval pattern: 1 - 1 - ½ - 1 - 1 - 1 - ½. However, in order to keep the sequence of whole steps and half steps the same starting on any note except C, we must use chromatic signs (sometimes called accidentals). The number and type of chromatic signs we use (i.e. sharps, flats, double-sharps or double-flats) are determined by the starting note of the scale. Examine the scale below beginning on F:

This scale is called an F major scale because the first note is F and the pattern of intervals used is for a major scale. Notice that the fourth note in the scale has a flat in front of it. Because the note on the third line of the treble clef is called B, when we place a flat in front of the note, we call it B-flat. A flat in front of a note lowers the pitch by one half step. The interval between A and B is a whole step. Therefore, the interval betwen A and Bb is a half step. Look at the keyboard. You notice that A and Bb are adjacent notes (a half step). The only other half step in the F major scale is the naturally occurring half step between E and F.

The following scale is called a G major scale because the first note is G and the intervals used are those for major. In this case, the seventh note of the scale, an F, has a sharp in front of it, thus making it an F sharp. A sharp in front of a note raises the pitch by one half step. The only other half step in the G major scale is the naturally occurring half step between B and C.

Again, on the keyboard, notice that the half steps occur between adjacent keys, in this case F# and G and the naturally occuring B and C.

The important point to remember is that you can construct a major scale on any note as long as you follow the same pattern of whole steps and half steps and do not repeat any letter names. Now it's time to practice constructing major scales. Click the next button at the bottom right to begin.
