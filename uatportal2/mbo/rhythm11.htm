RHYTHM

Duplet

There is one exception to the system used for determining the durations associated with a plet. The exception is the duplet (division into two parts in compound meter). As you can see in the example below, the duplet uses eighths notes. It would logically follow from the procedure that we have used for determining the durations for plets in the examples above, that because 2 falls between 1 and 3, we should look at the duration associated with “1” for the duration value of the plet. However, “1” is the beat itself and is a dotted quarter-note. So, we turn to the durations associated with a division into three. In this case, the durations associated with 3 are eighths. There is another important distinction about the duplet. In the case of all of the other plets, the durations are a little shorter than the regular durations. In the case of the duplet, the durations are a little longer than the regular durations. If you take a superficial look at the duplet and quadruplet below, since both patterns equal the dotted-quarter note, it would seem that two eighths = four eighths. But of course we know that that is not the case. The eighths in the quadruplet are worth a little less than real eighths and the eighths in the duplet are worth a little more than real eighths.

To review, the regular divisions and subdivisions in simple meter divide or subdivide the beat into 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, etc., parts. The regular divisions and subdivisions in compound meter divide or subdivide the beat into 3, 6, 12, 24, etc., parts. Any division or subdivision of the beat that is not a regular division or subdivision is nonregular and is represented by a plet of some kind.
