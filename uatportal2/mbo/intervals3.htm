INTERVALS

Identifying intervals using the major scale

There are several methods for identifying or writing intervals on a staff. One method involves memorizing the whole and half steps in the chart on the previous page. However, there are simpler methods that are less prone to error. The best of these uses the major scale.

The general name. Recall that each degree of the major scale has a number beginning with 1 and ending with 8 (the octave). Each of these numbers also represents the general name of an interval relative to the starting note (tonic) of the scale. For example, note number 3 (E in a C major scale) forms the general interval of a third. Likewise, note number 2 (D) represents a second, note number 4 (F) a fourth, and so on. As seen earlier, you can even have a general interval of a 1st, which is called a unison. For this reason, when you count lines and spaces, always begin with the bottom note as number 1.

The quality name. In addition to having a general name, each interval has a quality name. There are seven quality names for intervals: double-diminished, diminished, minor, major, perfect, augmented, and double-augmented.

In a major scale, the quality name of the interval is always major or perfect.
Look at the C major scale below and examine the names of the intervals relative to the tonic.


From C to C (Scale degree 1 to degree 1) = 0 steps or a perfect unison (P1)
From C up to D (Scale degree 1 to degree 2) = 1 step or a major second (M2)
From C up to E (Scale degree 1 to degree 3) = 2 steps or a major third (M3)
From C up to F (Scale degree 1 to degree 4) = 2½ steps or a perfect fourth (P4)
From C up to G (Scale degree 1 to degree 5) = 3½ steps or a perfect fifth (P5)
From C up to A (Scale degree 1 to degree 6) = 4½ steps or a major sixth (M6)
From C up to B (Scale degree 1 to degree 7) = 5½ steps or a major seventh (M7)
From C up to C (Scale degree 1 to degree 8) = 6 steps or a perfect octave (P8)
